We are eusocial insects of the family Formicidae /fɔrˈmɪsɨdiː/ and, along with the related wasps and bees, belong to the order Hymenoptera. We evolved from wasp-like ancestors in the mid-Cretaceous period between 110 and 130 million years ago and diversified after the rise of flowering plants. More than 12,500 of an estimated total of 22,000 species have been classified. We are easily identified by our elbowed antennae and the distinctive node-like structure that forms our slender waists. We form colonies that range in size from a few dozen predatory individuals living in small natural cavities to highly organised colonies that may occupy large territories and consist of millions of individuals. Larger colonies consist mostly of sterile, wingless females forming castes of "workers", "soldiers", or other specialised groups. Nearly all of our colonies also have some fertile males called "drones" and one or more fertile females called "queens". Our colonies are described as superorganisms because we appear to operate as a unified entity, collectively working together to support the colony.*

We are

We have colonised almost every landmass on Earth. The only places lacking indigenous ants are Antarctica and a few remote or inhospitable islands. We thrive in most ecosystems and may form 15–25% of the terrestrial animal biomass. Our success in so many environments has been attributed to our social organisation and our ability to modify habitats, tap resources, and defend ourselves. Our long co-evolution with other species has led to mimetic, commensal, parasitic, and mutualistic relationships. Our societies have division of labour, communication between individuals, and an ability to solve complex problems. These parallels with human societies have long been an inspiration and subject of study. Many human cultures make use of us in cuisine, medication, and rituals. Some of our species are valued in their role as biological pest control agents. Our ability to exploit resources may bring us into conflict with humans, however, as we can damage crops and invade buildings. Some of our species, such as the red imported fire one (Solenopsis invicta), are regarded as invasive species, establishing themselves in areas where they have been introduced accidentally.*

What we believe in

Interface

It is amazing to see how we shaped the world to fit our needs, especially in our daily urban life. It works: trains are scheduled, garbage is being collected, taxes can be paid online.

But sometimes a service can be ill perceived because of user experience. We work with the UX always in mind, since the interface should be part of the whole design.

Solving the UX is a matter of keeping contact with the citizens or the customers and thus, small things can have a huge impact.

Data

Data helps us understand and predict behaviors.

We predict behaviors from the context and not from personal information. This is efficient and ensures privacy by design. We think infrastructures should be the first things to adapt themselves, before citizens.

Data, seen as facts, are also the best way to challenge conventional thinking and status quo.

But data is a tool.

Openness

We crave for transparency because we want to be trusted.

We use and produce open knowledge in general (open data, open source, open hardware etc).

All our civic or environmental projects are completely open because we are interested in impact more than in profit.

Attitude

We are passionate about learning things and taking the unknown path. We believe this is how innovation is driven. We try to innovate in all we do, not only code or design but also in organisation or in the way we hire.

Working as hackers, we prototype fast to prove our solution works, and only then we become engineers to cope with all the "impossibles".

It takes grit and boldness to innovate and we focus on impossible projects to prove they aren’t.

6element

Using data to reduce flows towards waste centers

6element is a project aiming at reducing waste arriving in recycling centers. It can be seen as an interface between citizens and recycling centers. We designed a sensor that monitors both customer flows and filling levels for every bin - in real time.

Then a predictive model can tell you when you should go to which recycling center. The information is provided through a mobile app that crowdsources waste position in space and time so that reusers can take advantage of it.

Bordeaux3D

Displaying and interacting with the city in 3D in your browser

We wanted to make it easy to represent and interact with the city. We created a technology enbaling a precise model of a whole city accessible through the browser. Nothing to install, no loading time.

Not only this gives a playground to visualize infrastructures like transports in real time, but it is also the best way for the city operator to communicate with the citizens and adapt the cityscape to their needs and desires.
